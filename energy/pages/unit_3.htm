Our focus: considering ways to live within our present energy income from the Sun.

What will be covered

At the conclusion of this unit, you will be able to state the principle advantages and disadvantages of solar energy, and describe how solar energy is naturally enhanced by other renewable energy systems. You will also be able to discuss the basics of renewable biofuels, and their implications for nonrenewable resources such as soil, water, and fertilizers.

After this unit, you'll be able to:

  • State the principal advantages and disadvantages of solar power.
  • Describe how natural concentration improves the quality of renewable energy.
  • List some advantages that biofuels provide over other renewable energy systems.
  • Identify some of the negative consequences of biofuel production.

  This unit's reading

The Sun is expected to continue shining for billions of years to come, setting a gold standard of sustainability that no other major energy source can hope to match. The Sun supplies the energy for all plant life, and therefore is also the source of energy contained in both biofuels and fossil fuels. The Sun also powers the weather, through solar heating of the ocean and land surfaces. Hydroelectric and wind power are really just cleverly disguised versions of solar power.

This excerpt from the book "Geofuels: Energy and the Energy" looks in more depth at solar energy, which the ultimate source of nearly all renewable energy.

  Video lectures

Title Description
Earth’s Energy Income (7:41) Renewable energy systems depend almost exclusively on the present influx of sunlight to the Earth. The magnitude of incoming solar energy is immense, but its intensity is relatively low at any one location. Its utility can therefore be greatly increase through natural or artificial processes of concentration.
Making the Most of It (8:14) Wind power, hydroelectric power, and biofuels all provide natural concentration of the diffuse energy provided by sunlight. Winds are generated by the unequal solar heating of the Earth, and hydroelectric power derives from evaporation of the ocean. Biofuels concentrate solar energy by slowly storing it in a chemical form that can be released much more quickly.
Harvesting the Sun (5:09) The highest agricultural yields are obtained in areas that have a fortuitous combination of rich soils and adequate rainfall. Natural patterns of soil fertility therefore exert a first-order control on the viability of large-scale biofuel production.
Guest interview: Tina Nielsen, Great Lakes Bioenergy Research Center (5:13) Current generation biofuels rely primarily on the fermentation of corn and sugar cane. These feedstocks are convenient because the infrastructure to grow them already exists, and the technology for fermenting them is well established. Producing biofuels in this way has the potential to compete with food supplies however.
The Dark Side of Green (10:10) The energy contained in biofuels is renewable and free, but the means of capturing it are neither. Cultivation of biofuels crops requires the use non-renewable soil, water, and fertilizer resources, that are also vital for growing food. The use of non-renewable resources is multiplied by the fact that current generation biofuels produced from corn (maize) provide very little net energy, and therefore require cultivation of larger land areas.
Special topic: Gulf of Mexico Hypoxia (3:06) Note: this video will open in a new tab/window, and is not included on the Video Lectures page due to being externally hosted. Runoff of nitrogen fertlizers from the midwestern U.S. has been directly implicated with increased nitrogen content in shallow waters near the point where the Mississippi River enters the Gulf Coast. Nitrogen fertilization causes runaway productivity of photosynthetic microorganisms. The decay of these organisms depletes oxygen in the water below the surface, harming other sea life.

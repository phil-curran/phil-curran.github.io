Our focus: how using fossil fuels taps into our geologic savings account, which has accrued energy over a period of 500 million years.

What will be covered

At the conclusion of this unit, you will be able describe the major categories of fossil fuels, and how their use has evolved since the 19th century, and the implications of these changes. You will also be able to describe the basic process of hydraulic fracturing (“fracking”), and discuss its implications for future energy supplies and for the environment.

After this unit, you'll be able to:

  • Compare fossil fuel abundance to solar flux during the time they accumulated.
  • Explain how 21st century fossil fuel resources differ from the fossil fuels of the 19th and 20th centuries.
  • Describe the basic process of fracking, and discuss its implications.

  This unit's readings

Where do fossil fuels really come from? Put simply, coal, oil, and natural gas represent solar energy that was originally trapped by plants and stored in the Earth’s crust. Fossil fuels can be roughly subdivided according to whether they originated from land plants, such as trees, herbs, and grasses, or aquatic plants, primarily one-celled algae.

This excerpt from the book "Geofuels: Energy and the Energy" examines the origins of the major fossil fuel groups, and compares the Earth to a very large natural solar collector.

Optional reading:

The advent of hydraulic fracturing technology has resulted in a marked upswing in drilling activity in many regions, leading to a vigorous debate over its local economic and environmental impact. The impact of fracking extends beyond the areas being actively drilled, however, and can even affect regions that possess no fossil fuel resources of their own. One such area is the state of Wisconsin, which is located in a region of the U.S. that generally lacks deposits of coal, crude, oil, or natural gas. Wisconsin does possess extensive deposits of quartz sandstone however, which have proven vital to supporting the fracking industry. Due to its physical characteristics and the ease with which it can be mined, Wisconsin sand is highly sought-after as proppant: the material that props open hydraulic fractures during oil and gas production.

The following reading/slideset examines the geology and mining of Wisconsin frac sand, along with the impacts of its use. While this topic is of special interest to those living in Wisconsin, the broader impacts of fracking should be of interest to all.

  Video lectures

Title Description
The Geologic Savings Account (4:34) Fossil fuels provide us with a kind of energy savings account, that accrued over millions of years but that can be spent much more quickly. Currently we have access to three principal types of energy currency: coal and oil shale, conventional oil and gas, and low-permeability (unconventional) oil and gas.
Habitat of Hydrocarbons Part 1:
Coal and oil shale (7:50)		 Coal and oil shale are rocks that burn. Coal forms from the remains of land plants and represents the earliest fossil fuel to be used on a large scale, and also appears to be the most abundant. Oil shale forms from the remains of aquatic plants. It can be heated to artificially produce liquid oil, and known resources are very large. However it has seen little commercial development.
Habitat of Hydrocarbons Part 2:
Conventional oil and gas (8:04)		 Conventional oil and gas fields contain hydrocarbon fluids that were originally generated across a large geographic area. They have been naturally concentrated into relatively small, localized accumulations that are challenging to find, but relatively easy to exploit.
Habitat of Hydrocarbons Part 3:
Low-permeability oil and gas (7:09)		 Unconventional oil gas represent hydrocarbons that have never traveled far from their source. They are widespread and relatively easy to find, but difficult to extract due to the low-permeability of the rocks that contain them. Horizontal drill and hydraulic fracturing make extraction possible by reducing the distance that unconventional oil and gas must flow through the subsurface.
Fracking Impacts (7:34) The development of unconventional oil and gas requires drilling wells across much larger geographic areas than is typical for conventional fields. The process of fracking itself has not yet been shown to inherently problematic. Increased drilling itself can have an impact on the surface environment, however. Deep underground injection of waste water has also been shown to cause earthquakes in a some areas.
Fracking discussion with Tim Carr (14:14) Hydraulic fracturing has dramatically boosted the production of oil and gas in the U.S., and will likely do the same in other parts of the world. Its full potential has yet to be realized. The techniques used to optimize production and protect the environment have changed continuously, and will likely continue to do so. As with conventional oil and gas development, adequate regulation is important for limiting potential environmental problems.

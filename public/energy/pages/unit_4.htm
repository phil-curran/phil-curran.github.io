Our focus: examining our future energy prospects, which generally are brighter than some expect.

What will be covered

At the conclusion of this unit, you will be able to summarize expected future energy supply and demand patterns. You will also be able to explain what is meant by fossil fuel “reserves," and discuss the origin of nuclear fuels and the implications of their use. You will also be able to describe some energy energy technologies.

After this unit, you'll be able to:

  • Summarize expected energy supply and demand patterns through 2100.
  • Explain what is meant by the term “reserves” and discuss its implications for the future of geologic fuels.
  • Discuss the origin of nuclear fission fuels and the implications of their use.
  • Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of emerging energy technologies.

  This unit's reading

The idea that we are about to run out of fossil fuels has been with us nearly as long as fossil fuels themselves. A continual procession of prognosticators have foretold their imminent decline, based on the seemingly commonsense principle that reserves are finite but future demand is not. These predictions all share one feature in common: they have all failed. For better or worse, fossil fuel supplies have proven surprisingly resilient.

This excerpt from the book "Geofuels: Energy and the Energy" examines the reasons why have so consistently defied predictions for their demise.

Optional reading

Fossil fuel combustion unavoidably releases CO2 as a byproduct, but it does not necessarily need to escape into the atmosphere. It is possible instead to capture CO2 and permanently sequester it. Various options for doing this have been proposed, but the leading candidate is deep underground injection. Underground injection of CO2 has been routinely practiced for decades by the petroleum industry, as a way to enhance the recovery of oil from conventional reservoirs. The technology to geologically store CO2 therefore already exists.

Carbon capture and storage (CCS) works best when using point-sources of relatively CO2, avoiding the need to extract it directly from the atmosphere. Typical point sources might include coal-fired power plants, ethanol refineries, and cement plants. The required technology has been tested at a number of pilot projects. However, there are also a number of potential obstacles to the large-scale use of CCS.

The following reading/slideset examines the basics of CCS, along with some of its challenges.

  Video lectures

Title Description
7 Billion and Counting (5:02) World energy use is expected to keep growing rapidly during the next few decades, particularly in developing countries. The details of how this demand will be met are uncertain, and the future of fossil fuels is particularly controversial.
When Will the Well Run Dry? (10:38) Near-term depletion of fossil fuel reserves has been predicted repeatedly, starting shortly after their use began. Current production rates and estimated reserves are both the highest they have ever been however. This apparent paradox stems from the fact that reserves depend not just the natural occurrence of fossil fuels, but also on investment in their development and on available extraction technologies. The currently known reserves do not represent an ultimate physical limit.
The Nuclear Alternative: Uranium Resources (6:38) Nuclear power offers the opportunity for essentially unlimited expansion of electrical generating capacity, without substantial emission of greenhouse gases. Currently known reserves of uranium are relatively modest, but much larger quantities can potentially be made available. The chief impediments to such an expansion are high capital costs, public concern over reactor safety, and the fate of spent nuclear reactor fuel.
The Nuclear Alternative: Reactor Safety. Discussion with Prof. Michael Corradini (4:55) Nuclear power provides reliable base-load electricity 24 hours a day, without significant greenhouse gas emissions. The public image of nuclear has been strongly influenced by a handful of prominent failures, most recently involving the Fukushima Daiichi plant in Japan. The overall safety of nuclear power has been very good however.
The Nuclear Alternative: Waste Disposal. Discussion with Prof. Michael Corradini (6:09) Current generation nuclear reactors use only a small amount of the uranium originally present in their fuel, and create radioactive waste that must be effectively stored for at least 1000 years. Effective solutions have been developed for long-term storage of spent nuclear fuel, although their implementation varies by country. In the future, “fast neutron” reactors hold the promise to use uranium more efficiently, decreasing the need for long term waste storage.
Biofuels in the Present: Discussion with Dr. Tina Nielsen (8:50) The production of ethanol from grasses and other cellulosic feestocks promises to greatly enhance the practicality of biofuels, but commercial implementation of this technology is challenging. Researchers are making rapid progress on this and other problems associated with biofuels however, which has the potential to change the energy landscape.
Course Summary (6:42) All energy systems are connected, due their universal use of finite Earth resources. This fact provides a unique, unifying perspective, from which the fragmented parts of the energy and climate debate can be critically evaluated. The future is generally hopeful; future technological advances will continue to expand our energy options and mitigate the impacts of energy use. There are no truly “green” solutions however; instead we will need to choose between the competing compromises.

Hydraulic Fracturing

Hydraulic fracturing requires injecting fluids into the subsurface at pressures high enough to exceed the failure strength of rocks. In order for a fracture to open, the pressures must also be high enough to overcome the confining pressure that is being exerted by the weight over overlying rocks, usually at depths of 1000 meters or more below the land surface.

This situation is directly analogous to inflating a balloon beneath the surface of the water in a swimming pool or bathtub. The volume of the inflated balloon represents the volume of an open fracture (although its shape may be different), and the water pressure at the depth where the balloon is inflated is analogous to the confining pressure exerted by rocks at the depth where a fracture opens.

For this part of the activity, you will subjectively measure the pressure required to inflate a balloon under two different conditions: at surface atmospheric pressure, and submerged beneath 50 cm of water.

Materials needed:

  • A party balloon
  • Rubber bands, duct tape, or string
  • About 1 meter length of plastic pipe or hose
  • Filled bathtub, jacuzzi, swimming pool, or other standing body of water

Procedure:

  1. Stretch the opening of the balloon over one end of the pipe or hose.
  2. Secure the balloon to the pipe or hose end using rubber bands, tape, or string.
  3. Blow into the other end of the pipe or hose, noting the amount of effort required to inflate the balloon.
  4. Now submerge the balloon beneath 50 cm of water (or as deep as you can get it) and blow into the tube or hose, noting the amount of effort required to inflate the balloon.

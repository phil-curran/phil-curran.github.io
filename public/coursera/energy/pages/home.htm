Overview

Energy use and its consequences motivate some of the most contentious public debates of our time. These issues are intrinsically complex, and embody difficult tradeoffs between physical, economic, and political realities. All energy systems share a common dependency on finite Earth resources, although these dependencies may not always be apparent.

This 4-unit course explores these issues through short video lectures, readings, activities, and discussions that will guide learners on an exploration of present and future energy systems, focusing on how the evolution of the Earth has shaped energy tradeoffs.

You will learn how to apply three main themes to the evaluation of any proposed energy solution:

  1. Energy quality is more important than quantity. We have plenty of energy available; what is important is our ability to make practical use of it.
  2. Technology changes reality. The history of energy use if one of continuous technological innovation, which continually redefines the possible.
  3. There is no free lunch. No solution is entirely “green”; any energy system scaled up to satisfy a significant share of our demand will bring economic and environmental consequences of its own.

Course Content and Activities

Each unit will include one required reading, between 5-10 short video lectures, a brief multiple-choice quiz, an activity, participation in the online discussion board, and a “Digging Deeper” list of additional readings and resources related to the weekly topic.

The required readings are brief excerpts (6-7 pages each) from the book “Geofuels: Energy and the Earth” by the course instructor, Alan Carroll. Note: these excerpts are provided only for the use by students in this course – they should not be reposted or redistributed outside of the course.

You can purchase your own copy of "Geofuels: Energy and the Earth" from Cambridge University Press or Amazon.com.

The lecture videos are usually between 3 and 12 minutes long. An interview or discussion with a guest expert will be included each week.

Quizzes help you review what you have learned

The activities provide ways for you to explore and reflect on your own energy use, and also to complete hands-on experiments that further demonstrate processes or concepts described in the readings and videos. You may be prompted to respond to discussion board questions specifically related to an activity.

The “Digging Deeper” resources and materials are optional, provided for you to explore if you wish to research more about a particular topic.

While you can complete the materials in any order you choose, we highly recommend that you begin with the required reading and then watch the lecture videos before proceeding with the quiz and activities.

 

Participation

You can explore and learn at your own pace, and you have the option to choose how to spend your time in the course. You may choose to select only the topics and activities that interest you the most, or you may choose to complete all of the work.

To complete the entire course, you can expect to spend between 2-3 hours each week. You will need to:

  1. Explore the course readings, additional information and resources.
  2. Watch the video lectures.
  3. Complete each of the quizzes.
  4. Complete each activity.
  5. Participate in the online discussions.
    You'll be answering questions for some of the activities using the course discussion board. You can also join an existing conversation that is of interest to you by commenting on others' posts, or start a new thread with your own question or comment related to the content of the course. There is no specific word count or number of posts required - the goal is to thoughtfully engage in a discussion about the course topics.
